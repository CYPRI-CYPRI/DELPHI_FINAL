Key Activities and Roles of Clinical Pharmacists Working in Mental Health

This page presents the final consensus-based framework developed through a European modified e-Delphi study conducted within the Mental Health Special Interest Group of the European Society of Clinical Pharmacy (ESCP).

Study overview

The aim of the study was to establish a structured, consensus-based framework of key clinical activities for clinical pharmacists working in mental health across Europe. The study involved experts from 13 European countries.

Background

Clinical pharmacy services in mental health have developed heterogeneously across Europe. Although competency frameworks exist, a clearly structured and agreed European list of core clinical activities in routine mental health pharmacy practice has been lacking.

What this page shows

Below is the final set of consensus-based criteria organised into seven domains. These domains reflect the main areas in which clinical pharmacists contribute to safe, effective, and person-centred psychopharmacotherapy.

Final consensus domains

The final framework comprises seven domains covering medicines optimisation, physical health monitoring, person-centred care, interprofessional collaboration, advanced roles, governance, and research or service development.

D1
Medicines Optimisation
⌄

1. Conduct comprehensive medication reviews for individuals with mental illness including pharmacotherapy across specialties. This includes reviewing the appropriateness of the following prescribing parameters relating to each medication: (i) indication, including optimised selection within the drug class; (ii) duration of treatment; (iii) potential for drug-drug and drug-disease interactions; (iv) proactive management, including prevention of adverse drug reactions and other medicine-related problems. (1a)

2. Prioritise structured medication review in older adults and others with multimorbidities (e.g., cardiac, renal, or hepatic impairment), polypharmacy, frailty, or use of higher-risk psychotropic medicines (e.g., high-dose antipsychotics, lithium, anticholinergic drugs, benzodiazepines) to reduce treatment burden, prevent medication-related harm, and improve quality of life. (1b)

3. Use validated tools (e.g., Beers, STOPP/START) within comprehensive medication reviews to recommend safer alternatives and minimise the risk of prescribing cascades. (1c)

4. Provide medication reviews within both primary and secondary care settings. (1d)

5. Ensure medication reconciliation is completed. Medicines reconciliation includes ensuring an accurate and complete medication history is taken, identifying and resolving discrepancies, and reviewing pharmacotherapy in collaboration with the multidisciplinary team. Reconciliation may be repeated in community, primary care settings, and at transition points with follow-up reviews added as needed to ensure safe and coordinated care delivery. (1e)

6. Provide seamless care, including during admission and transitions of care, including discharge. (1f)

7. Support adherence through tailored interventions and facilitate shared decision-making within consultations (e.g., support with long-acting injectable antipsychotic administration, management of adverse effects, etc.). (1g)

8. Manage clinically significant drug interactions (including drug-drug, drug-gene, and drug-disease, etc.). (1h)

9. Optimise use of high-risk psychotropics (including clozapine and long-acting injectable antipsychotics/high-dose antipsychotics), including adherence to physical health monitoring requirements. (1i)

10. Optimise the prescribing of psychotropic medication using pharmacokinetic and pharmacogenomic tools, as appropriate. (1j)

11. Make therapeutic substitutions where appropriate (e.g., formulary limitations, medicine shortages) ensuring equivalent efficacy and safety. (1k)

12. Provide interventions at multiple points of care, including admission and transitions of care, including discharge. (1l)

D2
Physical Health Monitoring and Interventions
⌄

13. Recognise and flag physical–mental health interdependencies relevant to pharmacotherapy (e.g., medication-related metabolic effects) and recommend appropriate assessment, review, or referral within the multidisciplinary team. (2a)

14. Recommend and implement physical health and lifestyle interventions for people with mental illness, including smoking cessation services, weight management, preventive screening programmes, and protocols supporting individuals with somatic comorbidities or medication-related risks (e.g., clozapine). (2c)

15. Use relevant tools, where available, to support identification of physical health risks and to guide recommendations for modifiable lifestyle factors. (2d)

16. Support monitoring and optimisation of chronic conditions in people receiving psychotropic medication by identifying medication-related risks, prompting appropriate follow-up, and collaborating with the multidisciplinary team. (2e)

D3
Person-Centred Care and Counselling
⌄

17. Provide person-centred pharmaceutical care in relation to medicines, involving individuals (and, where appropriate, families or carers) in shared decision-making about pharmacotherapy within the multidisciplinary team. (3a)

18. Provide medication counselling and medicines-related education to the people using them, including families or carers, supporting understanding, adherence, and shared treatment goals, while respecting individual cognitive and health literacy needs. (3b)

D4
Interprofessional Collaboration and Care Coordination
⌄

19. Integrate into multidisciplinary teams to provide expert pharmaceutical input. (4a)

20. Collaborate across care settings with pharmacists (hospital, community, general practice) and other healthcare professionals (e.g., psychiatrists, GPs, nurses, geriatricians, social workers, occupational therapists) to ensure safe and effective treatment and continuity of care, particularly during transitions of care (including admission, discharge, and primary care). (4b)

21. Document and communicate care plans, actions, and outcomes in a timely manner to support collaborative decision-making among the team. (4c)

22. Engage in professional networks and representative groups at local, national, and international levels to support service development and professional advocacy. (4d)

23. Contribute, where relevant and feasible, to discussions on funding or reimbursement models to demonstrate the value of mental health pharmacy services within the healthcare system. (4e)

D5
Advanced and Extended Roles
⌄

24. Facilitate medicines-focused group-based interventions (e.g., education about psychotropic medicines, adherence support, and management of adverse effects), where appropriate within scope of practice. (5e)

25. Offer community post-discharge services, including medicines reconciliation, outpatient medication reviews, and adherence support. (5f)

26. Optimise the use of new technologies to support medication-related decision-making (e.g., clinical decision-support systems, electronic prescribing tools, medication safety software, pharmacogenomic decision tools). (5g)

D6
Administrative and Governance Activities
⌄

27. Manage references and monitor high-risk medications to ensure safe use. (6a)

28. Conduct audits of prescribing practices and adherence to best practice standards. (6b)

29. Contribute to formulary decision-making regarding approved medications. (6c)

30. Develop and review clinical practice guidelines, clinical pharmacy guidance, and decision-making tools to support safe and effective prescribing in mental health. (6d)

31. Implement local and national practice recommendations, where relevant (e.g., medication-related monitoring protocols) across clinical teams and within the service. (6e)

D7
Research, Education, Service Development and Quality Improvement
⌄

32. Lead or participate in research, including clinical trials relevant to pharmacy services in mental health. (7a)

33. Collaborate within academic and professional networks to conduct research relevant to mental health pharmacy. (7b)

34. Provide education and mentorship to advance evidence-based mental health pharmacy practice. (7c)

35. Undertake quality improvement and service development projects. (7d)

36. Establish and monitor quality indicators to demonstrate the effectiveness of mental health pharmacy services. (7e)

37. Teach clinical pharmacy in mental health, including within undergraduate and postgraduate programmes, and among pharmacists and other healthcare professions. (7f)

38. Supervise undergraduate research, including thesis/dissertation supervision. (7g)

39. Conduct projects in mental health pharmacy to support innovation, service development, and reimbursement models. (7h)

40. Disseminate results associated with service evaluation to relevant stakeholders, including healthcare leaders, policymakers, and the public. (7i)
